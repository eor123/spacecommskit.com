01 // Theory of Operation

HOW A MARX GENERATOR WORKS

The Marx generator solves a deceptively simple problem: how do you generate very high voltage when your power supply only produces moderate voltage? The answer, conceived by Erwin Marx in 1924, is as elegant as it is powerful — charge capacitors in parallel, switch them in series.

During the charging phase, all capacitors charge to the same voltage through resistors. When the first spark gap fires — triggered either by the voltage reaching the breakdown threshold or by an external trigger — it places the first two capacitors in series. This doubled voltage causes the next spark gap to fire, which places three capacitors in series, and so on down the cascade until all stages have fired and the full multiplied voltage appears across the output.

Peak Output Voltage
V_out = n × V_charge
Where n = number of stages, V_charge = charge voltage per capacitor
For this build: V_out = 6 × 5,000V = 30,000V theoretical
Practical output is typically 70–85% of theoretical due to spark gap losses

   ├───┤├───┤├──┬───────┤├───┤├──┬───────┤├───┤├──┬─── OUTPUT+
              │  SG1            │  SG2            │  SG3
              ├─────────────────┘                 │
   R2    C2   │         R4    C4         R6    C6  │
   ┤├───┤├────┤  ┬───────┤├───┤├──┬───────┤├───┤├──┘
              └──┘  SG = SPARK GAP
  GND ───────────────────────────────────────────── OUTPUT-

  CHARGE: All C charge to V_charge in parallel through R network
  FIRE:   SG1 breaks down → C1+C2 in series → 2V across SG2
          SG2 fires → adds C3+C4 → 4V across SG3
          SG3 fires → all 6 caps in series → ~6V at output

The charging resistors serve two critical functions: they isolate the capacitors during charging so they all charge to the same voltage, and they limit the charging current. The resistance values are a tradeoff — too high and the charging time becomes impractical, too low and the resistors dissipate excessive power and may interfere with the discharge cascade.

02 // Build Description

HOW WE BUILT IT

V1 started as a proof of concept — can we build a working Marx generator from commercially available components, with a 3D-printed frame, that reliably produces visible arcs? The answer is yes, with some important lessons learned that drove the V2 redesign.

  • 01
    CAPACITOR SELECTION
    Each stage uses a high-voltage ceramic or film capacitor rated well above the operating voltage — minimum 2× safety margin on voltage rating. The energy stored per stage is E = ½CV², so capacitance and charge voltage together determine the output energy, not just the voltage. More capacitance means more energy per discharge but also longer charging time.
  • 02
    SPARK GAP CONSTRUCTION
    The spark gaps are the critical and most temperamental component. Each gap consists of two rounded electrodes with a precisely set air gap. The gap distance determines the breakdown voltage — too narrow and the gaps pre-fire during charging, too wide and the cascade fails to propagate. We used brass rod turned to rounded tips to minimize field enhancement effects at the electrodes.
  • 03
    CHARGING RESISTORS
    High-voltage resistors are not standard components. We used series strings of standard resistors to achieve the required resistance and voltage rating. The resistors must be physically spaced to handle the voltage across them without surface flashover — this is where the 3D-printed frame geometry matters.
  • 04
    3D PRINTED FRAME
    The frame was printed in PETG — better temperature resistance than PLA, adequate dielectric properties for this voltage range. The critical design constraint is creepage distance — the surface path length between conductors at different potentials. Rule of thumb: at least 2mm per kV for indoor use in clean conditions. The STL files include the frame geometry with integrated component mounting positions and adequate creepage paths.
  • 05
    POWER SUPPLY INTERFACE
    The Marx generator needs a DC high-voltage supply for charging. V1 was tested with a laboratory HV supply. The charging supply must be current-limited — the Marx fires fast and the charging supply must not be damaged by the discharge event propagating back through the charging circuit. A high-value series resistor between the supply and the Marx provides this protection.

03 // Test Results

WHAT HAPPENED WHEN WE TURNED IT ON

First fire was at reduced charge voltage — 2kV per stage, targeting ~12kV output. The cascade fired on the third attempt after gap adjustment. The discharge is fast — the arc duration is in the microsecond range, which means you hear it before you see it. The acoustic crack is startling even when you're expecting it.

✅
CONFIRMED WORKING Consistent cascade firing achieved after gap distance optimization. Arc length at full charge voltage confirmed in line with theoretical output. Full test documentation including measured output and arc photography available in the Patreon dossier.
⚠️
V1 LIMITATIONS — WHY V2 EXISTS Gap consistency was the primary challenge. Hand-adjusted gaps drift between sessions and require readjustment. Triggering is probabilistic rather than deterministic. The V2 redesign addresses this with improved gap geometry and a more rigid frame that maintains gap distance more reliably. See HV-002 for the full story.
04 // Next Steps

FROM V1 TO V2

V1 proved the concept and taught us everything we needed to know to build V2 properly. The lessons from gap geometry, frame rigidity, and component layout are all incorporated into the HV-002 design. V1 remains operational and is a great starting point for anyone new to impulse voltage work — the simpler design is easier to understand and troubleshoot.
